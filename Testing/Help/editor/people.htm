Adding People

Placing and editing people is what allows you the greatest influence over your level.  It is what can make your level more difficult, require more skill, or just blow people away.

People includes both enemies and civilians.  In most cases, how a person will act depends upon their selected weapon and logic table.

Placing People

To place a person, select person from the place objects... list box.  Move the cursor into the edit window and click them in.  It always defaults to a grenadier, who has a grenade as his weapon, and uses "default.lgk" as his logic table.  Each type of person differs in their look as well as their speech.

TIP: The names that follow each type of person indicate their speech pattern.  For example, Gunner Lisa, indicates that this person is a woman.  The names are the actually the name of the person that recorded the voice!

Editing People's Attributes

To edit a person, double click on them.  A dialog will appear that controls all of their attributes.  On the left hand side is a list of various personae.  These alter the look and set of dialog the enemy will use.  On the right is the list of available weapons.

TIP: Any type of person can use any type of weapon.   However, the animations included for each person may not match the type of weapon.   For example, the people in the table above with the *'s all use throwing animations with their weapons.  Therefore, they look better when using a weapon that is thrown, like grenades or dynamite.  They can throw heatseekers, but it may look a bit strange.

There are a couple of choices which may be helpful when designing your level. Log AI Activity causes the state of the person to be displayed while you are play testing your level.  It also creates a log file called logtablog.log in your Postal directory, which keeps track of the states of that person. Show Current State on Screen shows the logic, type, and weapon of the person you have created below them.

At the bottom of the dialog, you can select which logic table the person is using.   Click on the '...' button to bring up a list of all available logic types and choose a new one if you wish. (See below for more information on Logic Tables.)  When you're done, click OK.  Now the changes you've made will take effect.

 

Logic Tables

These control a variety of behavioral attributes such as: what to do when shot, how aggressive to be, does the enemy care if his friends nearby get shot, does he respond to Pylons, does he roam or guard, etc.

TIP:  Some of the logics require bouys in order to function properly.  If you have changed the logic of a person, make sure to add at least two bouys.

The following are examples of logic tables included with the game. If you are truly adventurous, you can actually create your own logic tables, but be forewarned, this is not for the feint-of-heart.

Logic Type

berserk The person will act normally until shot and then becomes "fodder".
bouy The person will walk around until they find a pylon and then they will use it.

fodder Detects player as soon as the level begins and attempts to engage in battle at all costs.
guard Stands his ground unless shot, then he'll attack/and or try to move to a safer spot to shoot at player.

lurkfar Stands his ground until player gets within a certain distance.
lurkmed Closer version of "lurkfar".

Pylons

Pylons provide additional means to create specific behavior for enemies.  To place pylons, select them from the object list and then click on the background to place. Each pylon you placed will be numbered in order.  Once you place a pylon, double click on it to edit its attributes, and double RIGHT click on it to set its trigger region.  After creating the pylons for an enemy, you must edit its properties and enter a Start and End pylon under Special Case.  Pylons can be set to the following:

Pylon Type Action
pop out This type defines a start point for an enemy to run out, shoot, then run back to the start point.  Once this type has been chosen, press OK and a second dialog will ask for the number of the target pylon.  The target pylon must be set with "no message".  This requires an enemy with default, popout, or pop_only logic table. (The logic table you choose will determine how easily the enemy can be "distracted" from following the pylon's command.)
Run & shoot This type requires that you set up two pylons, each referencing the other, to provide a path, which the enemy will run back and forth along, shooting at you simultaneously(strafing as he runs) when you step onto one of their trigger regions.   This requires an enemy with default logic.
Safe Enemies will see these as "cover" and try to hide there if they are wounded and have a logic table that allows them to notice pylons.


Editing Pylon Trigger Regions

Double RIGHT click on a pylon and a bounding square will appear centered on the pylon.   Use the arrow keys to position this square anywhere on the background art that you'd like(shift, ctrl, alt all speed up the square's movement).  When you move the cursor into the square, a smaller square appears.  This is your "brush", use it to paint in the spots within the bounding square that you want to act as trigger regions for the pylon's command. Increase the size of the brush with the "+" key, or shrink it with "-".  Hit "Esc" when you're done.

TIP: When you edit the pylon trigger region in the Pop out or Run & Shoot types, make sure to edit the regions of all pylons.  If you don't get the desired response from an enemy, it could be because the second pylon was not edited properly.
